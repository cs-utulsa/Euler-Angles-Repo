Introduction to Vectors

Definition of a Vector

A vector is a quantity that represents both magnitude and direction. It is geometrically represented as a line segment with an arrow at its head indicating the direction.

Vectors are often denoted with boldface letters or with arrows over the letters \((\vec{a})\). The magnitude of the vector \(\boldsymbol{a}\) would be represented as such: \(\|\boldsymbol{a}\|\). In the picture below, vector \(\boldsymbol{a}\) is represented by the blue arrow. Its magnitude is represented by the red arrow, while its direction is represented by the green arrow. A number that is not a vector is called a scalar, so the magnitude itself would be considered a scalar, as it is simply a quantity that describes the vector.

No matter where vector \(\boldsymbol{a}\) is positioned on the coordinate system, its magnitude and direction do not change, so the blue arrow could be picked up and moved to another place, and it would remain the same. However, if the arrow was stretched out further or the head was rotated around, then the magnitude and direction would change to reflect a new vector.

Vectors in the 3D Coordinate System

When a vector is expressed in terms of a coordinate system, it is written as a list of numbers known as coordinates. With the two-dimensional Cartesian coordinate system, a vector \(\boldsymbol{v}\) is represented by two coordinate points known as the \(x\) and \(y\) components: \(\boldsymbol{v}=(v_{1},v_{2})\). Looking back to the image above with the blue arrow representing vector a, it becomes evident that the blue line segment is really the hypotenuse of a right triangle with legs that are a length of 2 and 1.01. The magnitude then would be the length of that line segment, which can be calculated using the Pythagorean Theorem. \[\|\boldsymbol{a}\|=\sqrt{a^2_{1}+a^2_{2}}\] We can expand this idea to three-dimensional space where a vector has three coordinates now. The coordinates of a vector can be determined by placing its tail at the origin of the Cartesian coordinate system, like the green line segment below, and finding the coordinates of its head.

Just as with the two-dimensional coordinate system, the magnitude of a vector in three-dimensional space can be calculated by the Pythagorean Theorem. The equation to find the magnitude of vector \(\boldsymbol{a}=(a_{1},a_{2},a_{3})\) is as follows: \[\|\boldsymbol{a}\|=\sqrt{a^2_{1}+a^2_{2}+a^2_{3}}\] In lessons to follow, it will be useful to understand the three-dimensional coordinate system and vectors as key components behind representing the rotation of an object in space.
